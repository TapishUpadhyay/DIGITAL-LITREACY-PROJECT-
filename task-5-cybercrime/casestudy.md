# Cybercrime Case Study — Phishing

## What is Phishing?

Phishing is a type of cybercrime where an attacker impersonates a trusted entity — such as a bank, government body, or popular website — to trick victims into revealing sensitive information like passwords, OTPs, or credit card numbers.

---

## How It Typically Happens (Step-by-Step)

1. **Bait is created.** The attacker crafts a fake email, SMS, or WhatsApp message that appears to come from a legitimate source — for example, "Your SBI account has been suspended. Click here to verify."

2. **The victim receives the message.** The message creates urgency or fear (e.g., "Act within 24 hours or your account will be blocked"), pushing the victim to act quickly without thinking critically.

3. **The victim clicks the link.** The link leads to a fake website that looks identical to the real one — same logo, same layout, same colour scheme.

4. **Credentials are entered.** The victim enters their username, password, or OTP, believing they are on the real site.

5. **Data is stolen.** The attacker instantly captures the credentials and uses them to access the real account, transfer money, or sell the data.

6. **Damage is done.** By the time the victim realises, funds may have been transferred or personal data misused.

---

## Who is Usually Targeted?

- College students and young adults unfamiliar with online safety
- Senior citizens with limited digital literacy
- People who have recently made online transactions (targeted via fake delivery notifications)
- Job seekers receiving fake offer letters with malicious attachments

---

## Consequences

- Financial loss (money transferred from bank accounts)
- Identity theft and misuse of personal data
- Emotional distress and loss of trust in digital systems
- Legal complications if stolen credentials are used for further crimes

---

## Scenario Example (Realistic Fictional Case)

Priya, a first-year student at a central university, received an SMS claiming her scholarship disbursement was on hold. The message included a link to "update her bank details." The website looked exactly like her university's portal. She entered her net banking username and password. Within minutes, Rs. 12,000 from her savings account was transferred out. She had not noticed the URL was `scholarshipportal-update.in` instead of her university's official domain.
